# Our Mission & Vision

We do a lot of silly things at camp, but the underlying reason we are here is serious business.

**Mission Statement**
The mission of Scouting America is to prepare young people to make ethical and moral choices over their lifetimes by instilling in them the values of the Scout Oath and Law.

**Scout Oath**

On my honor I will do my best
To do my duty to God and my country

**Vision Statement**
Scouting America will prepare every eligible youth in America to become a responsible, participating citizen and leader who is guided by the Scout Oath and Law.

**Camp Lawton's Mission and Vision**
**Mission:** Camp Lawton transforms lives through safe, welcoming, and memorable outdoor experiences where Scouts learn by doing, staff lead through service, and everyone grows in character, skill, confidence, and connection.

**Vision:** Everyone who leaves the mountain carries forward the courage to lead, the character to serve, and a lasting sense that they belong in Scouting and in the outdoors.


# The Core Pillars of Summer Camp

An organized camping program like ours is an educational powerhouse that targets growth in four specific areas:

* **Physical:** Camp helps develop habits of healthy living through balanced meals and supervised physical activities.  
* **Mental:** Advancement programs offer challenges that build self-sufficiency and responsibility.  
* **Social:** The community of a summer camp helps build strong, morally rich people skills.  
* **Spiritual:** Spiritually rich time in nature helps develop a deeper understanding of a scout’s place in the universe.

# The Aims and Methods of Scouting

**The Aims of Scouting**

* **Character:** Developing integrity, responsibility, resilience, and the habit of making ethical choices.
* **Citizenship:** Learning to serve others and participate responsibly in the community, nation, and world.
* **Personal Fitness:** Building lifelong physical, mental, emotional, and spiritual well-being.
* **Leadership:** Developing the confidence and skills to guide, support, and inspire others.

**The Methods of Scouting**

* **Ideals:** Living by the values expressed in the Scout Oath, Scout Law, motto, and slogan.
* **Patrols:** Learning teamwork, responsibility, and leadership through small, youth-led groups.
* **Outdoor Programs:** Building skills, confidence, and appreciation for nature through outdoor adventure.
* **Advancement:** Growing through progressive challenges, skill development, recognition, and achievement.
* **Association with Adults:** Learning from positive adult role models who provide guidance, support, and encouragement.
* **Personal Growth:** Reflecting on experiences, setting goals, and steadily becoming a more capable person.
* **Leadership Development:** Practicing leadership through real responsibilities, decisions, successes, and mistakes.
* **Uniform:** Creating a visible sense of belonging, equality, commitment, and shared identity.


# What Makes A Staff?

A Camp Staff is more than a group of employees going about their assigned duties. The staff is a group of dedicated Scouts and Scouters who lead by example, set the tone for the attitude and excitement in camp, and are responsible for the lifetime of memories made during their weeks at camp.  
To get a good idea of what we are asking you to do, please pay close attention to the following four areas which will influence our Scouts during their visit to Camp Lawton:

* **Appearance:** Hygiene, uniform, and how you present yourself make an  
  impression. Before you speak, your appearance will have spoken.  
* **Attitude:** Stay positive and remember you are there to serve as well as  
  have fun. Try to have fun serving.  
* **Personality:** Patience, friendliness, humor, respect and enthusiasm; these traits make all the difference.  
* **Knowledge:** When teaching a Merit Badge or skill, the staƯ member should be an authority on the subject that he or she is teaching. In addition, the staƯ should be well versed in the structure and operations of Camp Lawton.

Remember, our goal is to provide a safe, educational, and fun environment for our Scouts. You play a major role in meeting those goals.

# Staff Positions

*“You know what the chain of command is? It’s the chain I go get and beat you with ’til you understand who’s in ruttin’ command here.” \- Jayne, Firefly*

To help you understand how our team operates, here is a quick breakdown of the camp hierarchy:

* **Camp Director:** The prime authority in camp.  
* **Program Director:** Reports directly to the Camp Director and oversees all program operations.  
* **Area Directors:** Report to the Program Director and manage specific sections of camp.  
* **Program Staff:** (Instructors, Junior Staff, and CITs—statistically, this is probably you\!) Report directly to their respective Area Directors.

Alongside the program team is the structural core of our camp that reports directly to the Camp Director: the **Ranger**, **Trading Post/Business Manager**, **Medic**, **Kitchen Staff**, and **Camp Commissioner**.

To look at it practically, camp operates in two main spheres:

* **The Camp Director manages the "Back Stage."** They run the operational side of camp, ensuring everything is done safely, efficiently, on budget, and up to national standards. It’s their job to guarantee everyone is fed, facilities are well-maintained, and the camp stays safe, secure, and fully compliant.  
* **The Program Director manages the "On Stage."** They run the actual camp experience—the merit badges, advancement classes, campfires, Funday, and the day-to-day energy of the general camp staff.

**Teamwork & Priorities**  
Since you report to the program side, does that mean you can ignore a request from the Ranger or the Camp Commissioner? Definitely not\!  
Camp is a massive team effort, and if a core staff member asks for your help, there is a good reason for it. As a general rule, always step up and lend a hand unless the request directly conflicts with your specific duties, personal ethics, or the law. Don't turn down a request without a valid reason. If you do run into a conflict, just check in with your immediate director to figure out what takes priority.  
*Note: Health and safety emergencies will always instantly override any other task or duty.*

**Our Open-Door Policy**  
As you will hear repeatedly, we maintain a strict open-door policy. If you have any issues, grievances, or personal concerns, please bring them to the administration so we can address them immediately. We are here to support you. Additionally, if you ever feel the need, you are always welcome and encouraged to take your concerns directly to the Catalina Council or the Council Executive Board.

**Age Requirements for Staff Positions**  
To comply with National Camp Accreditation Program (NCAP) and state labor guidelines, certain roles carry strict minimum age requirements.

* Camp Director & Program Director: Minimum age 21  
* Range Activities Director and Climbing Director: Minimum age 21\.  
* Area Directors: Minimum age 18  
* Instructors / Junior Staff: Minimum age 16  
* Counselors in Training (CIT): Ages 14-15.

**Counselors-in-Training (CITs) & Volunteers**  
Our Counselors-in-Training and volunteers are in a unique and vital position. To our campers and visitors, the distinction between these individuals and full-season staffers should be entirely invisible—they are staff members and deserve the exact same professionalism and respect as any other colleague.  
However, behind the scenes, it's crucial to remember the dual nature of their role. CITs are, in essence, “interns” participating in a **leadership development program**. They are the next generation of our team, and it is everyone's responsibility to mentor them effectively and ensure they are treated right.

**Guidelines for Mentoring CITs:**

* **Balance the Workload:** They are here to learn, not to be your "go-fers" or dedicated clean-up crew. Conversely, because they are still training, they should  NOT be left to teach a merit badge or lead a group entirely by themselves. Find the middle ground between treating them like children and treating them like fully autonomous employees.  
* **Strictly Observe Labor Laws:** strict federal and state labor laws apply to 14 and 15 year old staff. Their working hours are strictly limited to between 7:00 AM and 9:00 PM, with a hard cap of 8 hours per day.  
* **Encourage Exploration:** Their short-term rotations are designed to help them explore different departments. This is a deliberate strategy to help camp leadership identify where their individual talents shine the brightest.

By the end of their stay, these future leaders should walk away with a profound appreciation for the massive effort required to keep the mountain running. They are incredibly valuable to our daily mission right now, and they represent the absolute future of our staff legacy.

**Junior Staff**  
As older teenagers, this qualifies as a summer job. Lower paying than the alternatives, but far more fulfilling. Studies have found that summer camp work is a far better preparation for excelling at any workforce than those who just worked retail or food service. They learn interpersonal skills, teaching, food service, cleaning, professional behavior, procedural adherence, how to work through the toughest conditions… The list goes on. To hiring managers who know, a history of camp staff is a gold mine for new hires.

**Adult Staff**  
Spoiler alert: "Adult Staff" officially refers to anyone 18 and older. While many adults step into Area Director roles, that isn’t always the case. Aside from strictly adhering to \[Safeguarding Youth\] guidelines, the only real difference between Adult and Junior Staff should be an extra dose of maturity and leadership.

There is a common myth that turning 18 magically exempts you from the less glamorous parts of camp—like running logs, meal cleanup, shower duty, or being aggressively enthusiastic at campfires. **It does not**. It is neither fair nor appropriate to ask our youth staff to tackle chores that the adults are unwilling to do.

That being said, we recognize that aging is a trap. Around your 30th birthday, parts of your body start plotting against you, which means leading 20 minutes of high-intensity camp songs can become a genuine physical hazard. Because of this, adults do have a bit more leeway to opt out of certain activities if physical limitations (or a truly intense aversion) get in the way.

Keep in mind that core camp leadership (Administration, the Medic, Kitchen Staff, the Ranger, the Trading Post, the Commissioner, and Area Directors) will frequently have critical, behind-the-scenes tasks that pull them away from general staff activities. 

However, no matter what they tell you, "drinking coffee" does not qualify as a critical task.

# Staff Expectations

**What are my duties anyway?**  
I'm so glad you asked\! Camp Staff is much more than a regular job. So much so that calling it a “job” is woefully insufficient. It's a lifestyle. A calling. In the coming weeks, you will make friends, maybe enemies, probably a few frienemies. Heck, you might even fall in love (see the section on Fraternization)\! You'll do things you've never done and things you never thought you could do. You'll do tedious stuff, gross stuff, grueling stuff and painful stuff. It'll be really hard at times. But it’ll be worth it, I promise. 

**Here is the what we expect from you**:  
*(unless you've worked out a special arrangement with the Camp Director)*

* **Show Up & Speak Up:** Attend all meetings, trainings, and Staff Week. Bring your ideas\! A good idea is a good idea, no matter where you are on the organizational chart.  
    
* **Beat the Clock:** "On time" is late. Aim to be at least five minutes early to everything.  
    
* **Jump In:** Bring the energy. Participate in and help out with all camp-wide programs like flags, meals, campfires, and games.  
    
* **Be Respectful and Courteous:** Treat everyone, especially campers and visitors, with courtesy. For that matter, you probably know a specific set of 12 words that apply here.  
    
* **Use the Chain of Command:** Your Area Director is your go-to person. They’ll pass it up the ladder. You *can* absolutely speak to the "mysterious and god-like" camp admin, but following the chain keeps communication clear and gets problems solved faster.  
    
* **Don't Look (or Smell) Like a Slob:** Shower regularly, wear deodorant, brush your teeth, and swap out your dirty clothes. You might not have seven days' worth of fresh uniforms, but have backups. For the sake of your cabin-mates, please manage your funk.  
    
* **Be In Uniform:** See the \[Glossary\] for exact uniform standards.  
  **Class A (Field Uniform):** For Flags, campfires, chapel, and formal events.  
  **Class B (Activity Uniform):** For daily program duties.  
  **"Civvies" (Casual Wear):** Fine on Staff Hill out of camper sight, but keep it Scout-appropriate (the "Grandma test" applies).  
  **Water Bottle:** Yes, your water bottle is part of your uniform at all times in camp. Don’t forget it\!   
  *Note:* If you step off Staff Hill, you **must** be in at least a Class B. Your downtime isn't always guaranteed, so always be ready to change back into uniform on short notice.  
    
* **Fuel Up & Sleep:** Maintain your readiness by eating actual meals and getting enough sleep. It sounds basic, but exhaustion is the \#1 reason staff (and admin\!) crash out.  
    
* **Keep Your Quarters Clean:** We won’t do cabin inspections unless we need to. Please don't make us need to.  
    
* **Respect the Camp:** Take excellent care of all camp equipment and facilities as well as the forest itself. If respect for nature isn’t enough, remember that Camp Lawton exists on a lease from the Forest Service, and they can revoke it if we give them reason.   
    
* **Wear Your Name Badge:** Keep it visible on the top half of your body (lanyard or clipped) at all times so people know you're official Camp Lawton Staff. National accreditation guidelines require identification for all persons in camp in order to prevent intruders.    
    
* **Own It:** Your number one job is to serve the Scouts, the camp, and your fellow staff. Take personal responsibility for the success of camp. Even if it isn’t strictly in your job description, if something needs doing, step up\!  
    
* **Live the Ideals:** Abide by the Scout Oath, Law, and the rest of the Scouting ideals.

**"Other duties as assigned..."** This list doesn't cover every possible thing that might be asked of you. That would be one heck of a manual. Just come ready to roll with the punches, pitch in, and do whatever it takes to make this a successful summer at Camp Lawton.

**What can we expect from the Camp Administration?**  
Clearly we’re asking a lot of you. What kind of monsters would we be if we didn't give you something to expect of us? There's a whole \[Employment Information\] section that'll cover our responsibility to you as your employers, but let's talk about what we owe you as human people.

* **A (Figurative) Camp Family:** We might not all like each other 100% of the time, but we will always have your back. We aren't your parents, but we promise to step up and be the reliable adults you need, whenever you need us.  
* **An Open Ear:** Keeping Scouting America’s Safeguarding Youth guidelines in mind, you should always feel safe talking to camp leadership about absolutely anything on your mind.  
* **A True Safe Space:** Camp Lawton is your summer home, and we owe you a secure one. That means you have the right to work in an environment totally free from unfair expectations, bullying, harassment, abuse, and malicious pranks.  
* **Grace for Mistakes:** Effective management requires discipline, but we also know you're human. We won't come down on you unreasonably for a genuine mistake, and most minor lapses in judgment will earn a second chance. Just know that *serious* violations of camp rules will still be met with serious consequences.  
* **Fair Compensation:** You will be compensated promptly and fairly based on your position and employment agreement. Keep in mind that "compensation" doesn't always mean a paycheck, but we promise your hard work will always be genuinely appreciated and recognized.

# The Junior Staff Conundrum (Basic Ground Rules)

Under ideal conditions, the vast majority of Camp Lawton's staff is made up of Junior Staff and CITs between 14 and 17\. That of course presents this chilling challenge: we've got a bunch of hormonal mixed gender teenagers running this place\! How are we ever to survive?

On being hired, you’ll have signed a \[Code of Conduct\] with all the *official* rules, but let's just address a few key things with the blunt honest maturity I trust you can handle.

**Phones**  
I'm not going to be one of those people who blames all of society's woes on kids who won't look up from their smartphones. You've got lives on there. I get it. However, Camp Lawton needs you *here* if we're going to get what we need from you and if you're going to get anything out of the summer yourself. Keep it put away\! That goes for tablets and laptops as well (unless they’re approved for use as a program aid). We will begin the summer with no blanket ban on phones but any abuse and we will have to drop the hammer.

**Photography and social media**  
We encourage taking photographs and videos of camp. Ideally, use a dedicated camera device so we don’t run into any phone abuse issues. **Crucially though**, the sharing of scouts’ faces on social media can get *really* messy. You **must** run any content you intend to share by the Camp Director, or otherwise assume that sharing photos of scouts is strictly prohibited. It’s not censorship, we just don’t want to put any kids in harm’s way (or put the Council in legal jeopardy).

**Video Games and other recreation**  
Camp can be stressful and we all need a healthy release. In your limited off time, video games can be great for that. So can books, music, movies, TV, even card and board games. If you want to start up a *D\&D campaign*, I might even join you\!

**However… we're gonna have a problem if:**

1. You're too loud and are heard by scouts or leaders. It doesn't take much.  
2. You are late to or miss a program because of it.  
3. You are playing, watching, or doing something not in line with scout values. That means M rated games, R rated movies, card games with excessive language, violence or sexual content, etc. Cards Against Humanity is fun, but let’s please stick to Apples To Apples.

And leave your huge gaming rigs at home\! Yes, I’ve seen it, and it’s embarrassing.

**Drugs, Alcohol, Pornography**  
**No, no, and no.** *Duh*. If you don't know why, this may not be the right place for you. Yes, prescription drugs are fine as long as they're cleared and registered with the medic. No, I'm not going to debate with you what qualifies as pornography. And don't give me any “but weed is legal now” or “it's non-alcoholic beer.” This is a Scout camp\! Binge on Mountain Dew and sour gummy worms like the rest of us\!

**Fraternization**  
Spending a summer trapped together with a bunch of young people, there's one big taboo that we all come up against at one point or another. Given enough time, you’ll develop close bonds with your fellow staff. Sometimes *very* close. I've been there myself. Whatever your “trapped together / coming-of-age YA romance” love story may be, keep it out of camp. Why?

1. **Morality\!** You're Scouts. On staff, you have the responsibility of representing the pinnacle of scouting values, which don’t include PDA.  
2. **The Birds and the Bees\!** Holding hands leads to kissing. Kissing leads to French kissing. Yadda-yadda-yadda… and that's how babies are made. Let's not please.  
3. **Drama\!** Sure, smooching is fun\! But you're stuck here with these people. If something goes wrong, now we've all got to suffer.  
4. **Professionalism\!** As employees, fraternization can put us on the path to harassment, abuse, or assault complaints. That means people getting fired, lawsuits, civil or criminal trials, and entirely too much paperwork. Trust me, no one wants any of that and you getting handsy at camp just isn't worth it.

Sound fair? To be clear, I wouldn’t dream of getting in the way of your personal awakenings but as you learn in the Safeguarding Youth Training, there is zero tolerance for some of this stuff and I want to make sure you’re safe from any of that.

# Stress Management and Mental Stability

*“At some point, everything’s gonna go south and you’re going to say, this is it. This is how I end. Now, you can either accept that, or you can get to work. That’s all it is. You just begin. You do the math. You solve one problem and you solve the next one and then the next. And if you solve enough problems, you get to come home.” \- The Martian (2015), screenwriter Drew Goddard*

Let's be honest: staffing is HARD. Feeling stressed, overwhelmed, cranky, or even depressed at camp doesn't mean you are failing. It means your brain is reacting normally to a highly unnatural, high-stimulus environment. You are being tasked with managing the safety, education, and entertainment of a bunch of kids with very little time for yourself. That carries a massive cognitive load and can come at a brutal cost. We ask a lot of you, but not that.

Here are some tips to help you survive the summer with your sanity intact:

1. **Work the Problem \-** When the stress hits, it’s easy to spiral into catastrophic thinking ("I can't do this," "Everyone hates me," "This summer is awful"). Stop and work the problem. Run a self-diagnostic:  
   1. **Are you dehydrated?** It turns out, dehydration can mimic anxiety, spiking your heart rate and making you irritable. Have you had a Water Appreciation Moment lately?  
   2. **Are you fueled?** Surviving solely on energy drinks and sour gummy worms will crash your system. Eat actual food at the Dining Hall.  
   3. **Are you sleep-deprived?** Getting enough sleep can be tricky at camp when you’ve got a lot on your mind and a cabin full of other people with a lot on theirs. Lights Out isn’t just an arbitrary rule. It’s there to give you ample opportunity to get the sleep you need. Use that time from 10pm to 6am for sleeping\!  
2. **Use Your Siesta \-** Science has consistently confirmed that napping for a short time each day improves mental health and productivity. The daily schedule intentionally builds in a "Siesta" gap between lunch and the afternoon program. It is tempting to use that time for all the stuff you've been wanting to get done, but it is literally a “nap time” set aside as time for staff, scouts, and leaders to reboot. Go to your cabin, lie down, and let your brain stop processing external input for a bit.   
3. **Manage Sensory Overload \-** Camp is loud and bright and smelly. Between the incessant chatter of preteen kids, the cheers and songs, the WAMs, and the living in a cabin with a bunch of people you may or may not like, you rarely experience true peace. If you feel yourself getting overstimulated, find a quiet space out of sight of the campers where you can safely decompress. Listen to the breeze in the trees. Give your nervous system a chance to return to baseline. **Just remember your Area Director needs to know where you are and if you're under 18, you do still need a buddy**. Duct tape is available if your buddy needs some encouragement to silently enjoy the gentle sounds of the forest with you. (For legal reasons, that’s a joke.)  
4. **Tag Out (The Strategic Retreat) \-** If a scout is pushing your buttons, a lesson is completely derailing, or you feel a meltdown coming on, do not force yourself to power through until you snap. Tell your Area Director, "I need five minutes." Going somewhere *away* and taking a few deep breaths to reset is a hallmark of professional competence, not a sign of weakness.   
5. **Call in the Grown-Ups \-** If your mental health is slipping, if you are experiencing severe homesickness, or if a situation with a camper or cabinmate is too heavy for you to carry alone, go to a mature staff member that you trust. This goes for adults too, and the “grown up” doesn't have to be over 18\. You are *never* expected to go through *anything* alone. **Please don’t suffer in silence.**

**A reality check**  
As dangerous as it is to get overworked and burned out, it can also be all too easy to say “the Staff Handbook says I should prioritize my mental health, so I'm going to just stay in bed all week.” It happens to even the strongest of us and it's easy to spiral into apathy and lethargy. As miserable as you may be, don't give up. Talk to people. Cling to your purpose. Hiding from your responsibilities is never going to be the answer. Believe me, it just makes you feel worse. If you really need some time off, talk to the administration. We’ll work something out.

# Glossary

**Class A / Field Uniform:**

* An official Scout uniform shirt \- this can be Scouts BSA, Explorer, Venture, Sea Scouts, Cub Scouts, the official uniform of an international branch of World Scouting.  
* Scout pants/shorts \- NOT jeans\! They must be official uniform bottoms (Scouts BSA is olive green for instance, Venture is grey). Officially licenced skirts or other leg coverings are also acceptable. As the weather varies in camp, both shorts and pants are advisable.   
* Belt \- A scouting approved belt, whether an officially branded belt or a leather belt with a scouting related belt buckle, as long as it’s appropriate and indicates scouting in some way.  
* Closed toed shoes \- We recommend hiking boots or reliable sneakers that will hold up to significant abuse on the rough trails of camp. Closed toed shoes must be worn at all times in camp unless in your cabin or taking a shower (in which case we advise water shoes, flip flops, or crocs to prevent fungal outbreaks).  
* Socks \- We will not be picky about official Scout socks, but you should be wearing socks of some kind.  
* An undershirt \- While optional, the varying weather of the mountain makes layering our friend. The easiest undershirt is your staff shirt, making it easy to switch between Class A and Class B.  
* Hat (optional) \- if worn with the Class A, it must be scout related in some way.  
* Note: if you cannot afford a full and complete uniform, financial aid is available.

**Class B / Activity Uniform:**

* Camp Lawton Staff Shirt \- Primarily this year’s staff shirt, but past year staff shirts are acceptable. If necessary, any Camp Lawton shirt may be worn with administration approval. Other scouting related shirts may be approved by the Camp or Program Director.  
* Scout pants/shorts \- See above.  
* Closed toed shoes \- See above.

# Customer Service

One of the most important parts of your job on camp staff is making scouts feel appreciated and encouraging an atmosphere where scouts can thrive. In fact, Camp Lawton’s most powerful asset is its staff. Ask any scout who has gone to a larger camp to name five staff members. They won’t be able to. At Lawton, they'll know almost every one of you and have a story for each. That’s because at other camps, staff are just there to deliver the program. A camp our size allows us to build personal relationships with the scouts and give them unique experiences they won’t get anywhere else. To help you understand your role, here’s a few conceptual methods to follow. These were designed for corporate trainings, but they apply surprisingly well to us. 

**Dennis Snow’s customer service tips from Disney**

1. **Hire for Attitude**: Instead of hiring based solely on skills and trying to train personality later, prioritize hiring naturally friendly people and then train them on the necessary skills.  
2. **Everything Speaks**: Every detail, from the cleanliness of your facility to how quickly you respond to a request, sends a message to your scouts and their leaders about how much you value them. You must own every aspect of the experience.  
3. **It's a Stage**: Treat every employee as a cast member and every job as a performance. Maintain a clear distinction between the "onstage" experience for the customer and the "backstage" operations that should never be visible to them.  
4. **Map First**: Before training, map out every touchpoint through the lens of the customer. Identifying these points allows you to prevent service breakdowns before they occur.  
5. **Prioritize Systems over Scripts**: Excellence should be built into repeatable, reliable systems that anyone can follow, rather than relying on individual heroics or rigid, unnatural scripts.

**FISH Philosophy**

1. **Play**: Bring a lighthearted, spontaneous energy to your environment. This isn't about ignoring your responsibilities or enforcing “mandatory workplace fun.” It’s about finding creativity and joy in routine tasks. The work still gets done, but the atmosphere feels less rigid.  
2. **Make Their Day**: Look for ways to create a memorable, positive experience for someone else. It doesn’t require massive grand gestures or giving away free products. It is usually a small, unexpected interaction \- remembering a name, sharing a genuine laugh, or sincerely validating a customer's frustration.  
3. **Be There**: Give the customer (or your coworkers) your complete, undivided attention. This is often the hardest step to maintain. It means putting away distractions, pausing your mental to-do list, and actively listening. You are treating the person in front of you as the only thing that matters at that moment.  
4. **Choose Your Attitude**: Take total responsibility for the mindset you bring to work each day.You can't control angry customers, bad weather, or system outages. But you can decide how you react to them. It is an active, daily choice you make before you walk through the door, not a permanent personality trait.

**So how does that apply to me?**  
What it comes down to is that your attitude and how well each and every one of us takes ownership of the Camp Lawton experience will make all the difference in a scout's time at Camp Lawton. However long your summer is, the Scouts experience **one week** of camp, and something as little as you taking the time to ask them how their day is going or admire their boondoggle can stick with them for years.

# Preparing Your Program Area

You don't just want an area that functions. You want an area that is popular, effective, and thriving. Here is how you do it:  
**Running a Tight Ship (The Logistics)**

* **Anticipate Your Needs:** Don't wait until Tuesday to realize you're out of supplies. Plan ahead and make a list of everything your area will need for the entire summer and keep it updated every week.  
* **Be Resourceful:** You're not always going to have everything you need. That's just life at a summer camp with a budget. Improvise, adapt, and make do with what you have.  
* **Create a System:** Develop a foolproof system for how your equipment is used, tracked, and put away so nothing gets lost or broken.   
* **Lock It Down:** Secure and pack away all your equipment the second your operating hours are over.  
* **Keep It Clean:** Keep your space tidy, organized, and looking sharp at all times. A messy, trash-filled area is an easy way to look like an amateur.

**Bringing the Magic (The Experience)**

* **Know Your Stuff:** Become the undisputed expert on your subject matter. If you are teaching it, own it.  
* **Go Off-Script:** Drop in cool, weird, or interesting facts that aren't just copied out of the merit badge pamphlet.  
* **Keep It Hands-On:** Nobody comes to camp for a lecture. Make learning engaging by using games and interactive activities.  
* **Bring the Hype:** Use clever gimmicks, special events, and mini-contests to draw a crowd.  
* **Create a Vibe:** Give your area a distinct personality. Make it a unique, special destination that people actively **want** to hang out in.  
* **Keep It Simple, Make It Fun:** The golden rule of camp. If you remember nothing else, remember this.

**Merit Badge Lesson Planning**  
The Area Director’s job is to prepare a solid lesson plan for every Merit Badge or skill offered in your area *before* Staff Week even begins. A successful, engaging lesson plan involves:

**Structuring Your Week**

* Dividing the subject matter and requirements appropriately across your scheduled sessions.  
* Having a main theme or central idea for each day's learning.  
* Setting clear objectives for each lesson and communicating them directly to your Scouts.  
* Building a logical progression, making sure your lessons flow from foundational basics to more complex material.  
* Setting specific, clear due dates for when projects or assignments are due.  
* Spending the first few minutes of each day reviewing the material covered in the previous session.

**Bringing the Material to Life**

* Finding the most active, interesting, and hands-on methods to teach the subject.  
* Using all the tools available in your area to keep your students interested from start to finish.  
* Sharing your personal knowledge and experience to go far beyond the base requirements in your discussions.  
* Providing valuable information to the Scouts about potential career possibilities related to the subject.


**Using the Right Resources**

* Building your foundation strictly on official Scout publications.  
* Obtaining the latest version of the merit badge pamphlet to guarantee you have the most up-to-date requirements and information.  
* Taking advantage of digital resource guides, which can include useful videos and instructions to assist the Scouts.

**The Golden Rules of Requirements**

* You must follow the requirements exactly, making absolutely no deletions or additions.  
* You must require no more and no less than what the requirement specifically states.  
* If a requirement asks a Scout to "show or demonstrate," they must physically do it; just telling you about it is not enough.  
* You must ensure that each Scout individually and personally completes all of the requirements.

# Guiding Your Scouts: Empathy, Inclusion, and Leadership

A few years back, college professor David Gracey went viral on Tiktok for revealing he had a policy of giving universal accommodations. Instead of granting due date extensions and test redos to special needs students, he decided those same accommodations could benefit *all* his students. You will absolutely have neurodivergent scouts in your classes. Knowing how to talk to them will be crucial, but those same lessons will help you with *everyone*. 

Achieving cooperation and keeping your group engaged isn't about "control.” It's about connection, respect, and creating an environment where every Scout can thrive. Keep these psychological and practical principles in mind:

**Be the Thermostat, Not the Thermometer (Emotional Regulation)**  
If you lose your temper, you lose their respect. More importantly, youth, especially those who are neurodivergent or dealing with anxiety, will mirror your energy. If you escalate, they will escalate. Model the calm, grounded behavior you want to see.

**The Power of the Pause (Processing Time)**  
When you give directions, wait. Many neurodivergent individuals (such as those with ADHD or Autism) require a few extra seconds of "processing time" to translate auditory instructions into physical action. Give clear directions, then silently count to ten before repeating yourself.

**Always Explain the "Why"**  
Today's youth respond to autonomy and respect, not blind authority. "Because I said so" is a fast track to resentment. If you have a good reason for asking a group to do something, share it with them.

**Predictability Builds Trust**  
Be honest and consistent in what you say and do. For Scouts with special needs, predictability is a massive anxiety-reducer. If you promise something, follow through. If you make a mistake or get caught in an untruth, repairing that trust will be a long, uphill battle.

**Anticipate Needs (Proactive Leadership)**  
See possible outcomes before they happen. Is the dining hall incredibly loud? A Scout with sensory sensitivities might get overwhelmed. Anticipating these roadblocks allows you to pivot, offer accommodations, and keep your group moving smoothly. Stay a step ahead, but pace yourself so no one gets left behind.

**Remember That Behavior is Communication**  
Physical punishment, running laps, and demeaning tasks are strictly forbidden. When a Scout is "acting out," look beneath the surface. Is the Scout overstimulated? Hungry? Confused by the instructions? Find positive ways to lead by addressing the root cause, rather than just punishing the symptom.

**"Fair" Doesn't Always Mean "Identical"**  
Be consistent, but remember that true fairness means giving everyone what they need to succeed. A Scout with an intellectual disability or extreme social anxiety might need a different approach or accommodation than their peers.

**Tag in the Experts**  
Behavioral or disciplinary issues should always be referred back to the Scoutmaster. Unit leaders are the ones who know their Scouts' specific backgrounds, Individualized Education Programs (IEPs), and unique behavioral triggers. It is their job to manage their troop; it is your job to support them.

**Practice Radical Inclusion**  
Cliques and exclusive social groups can destroy a camp's culture. Strive to be an accessible, friendly face to everyone. Whatever you do, and wherever you go, make sure your words and actions signal that all Scouts, regardless of neurotype, ability, or background, are fundamentally safe and welcome with you.

# Gaming The Teaching System

**Teaching With E.D.G.E.**  
The official method Scouting America teaches everyone is the EDGE method. It’s a solid method.  
**Edge** stands for:

* **Explain**: Tell them what you’re gonna tell them.  
* **Demonstrate**: Show them how to do it.  
* **Guide**: Walk them through it, letting them try it a couple times.  
* **Enable**: Test and give them the tools to go on to take the skill with them into the future.

**Game Design Method (an alternate mindset)**  
The EDGE Method is awesome and all, **but consider this…**

The best **video games** have a very specific method to give players an enjoyable way to learn to play a game. It’s similar to EDGE, but a bit more fun. Let’s use Level 1-1 of the original Super Mario Bros. to illustrate.

1. **Explain**: Oh no\! Princess Toadstool has been kidnapped by the evil King Koopa\! **Let’s go save her\!**  
2. **Demonstrate**: The first thing you see in the level is a single slow moving Goomba, conveniently shaped like an arrow as if to imply **“you should jump here.”** After you’ve figured out how to target a jump, **you’re rewarded** with points, some floating bricks to break and some “?” block containing a power up and coins.  
3. **Guide**: As you go forward, you are shown a few **new elements** of the gameplay you’ll need to maneuver including pipes. One pipe you can go down for a bunch of free coins. If you choose to skip the pipe, there’s a hidden one up. Then you face your first first gap to jump. After the gap, things start ramping up. You get a second tier power-up (the fire flower) if you can survive the Goomba’s dropping down from the platform above. Then you meet your first Koopa Troopa that introduces a **new game mechanic** of the sliding shell.  
4. **Enable**: From there, things get more **complicated** and you will need to use all of the skills you’ve learned to complete the level. By the time you reach the flag, you are **fully prepared** to take on level 1-2.

So how can that possibly relate to teaching an 11 year old scout how to tie a **square knot**? Well try this out.

1. **Explain**: Oh no\! You need to tie down a parcel\! **Let’s tie a square knot\!**  
2. **Demonstrate**: You **introduce the concept** of a working end and a standing end of the rope. The working end is the one that does the work, the standing end just kind of plays along. Then show them the basic overhand, leading with the working end. Then another overhand, again leading with the working end.  
3. **Guide**: So now that they know the basics, they can **try it out** themselves. You can play around a bit with the **terminology**. Some people get the “working end vs standing end” method better while some people find the “right over left, left over right” easier to understand. **Everyone learns a little differently** so you want to experiment a bit to see what works for your students. **Get them doing it** and **be on the move** making each person feel comfortable. With knots, often **handling the anxiety** is most of the battle. It’s kinda like math.  
4. **Enable**: Now we actually **try tying some stuff down** with it. Shake things up, try out **different variations** on the premise of the square knot and challenge them. Did you know if you yank one end of a Granny Knot you get Two Half Hitches? Did you know you could tie a Square Knot with a bite around another byte? Did you know if you do that method differently, you can create a Thieves' Knot? The Square knot isn’t even its real name. It’s a Reef Knot, and the Ashley Book of Knots says it’s one of the **most useless knots** out there for anything actually important. Once you’ve shown them the amazing world of everything the Square Knot has to offer, not only are they going to walk away **fully understanding** the Square Knot, they now like knots and are **primed to learn** the next knots\!

# The Psychology of Effective Teaching

To be an effective instructor, you are doing more than just demonstrating a skill. You are helping to build new pathways in a Scout's brain. Keep these modern teaching strategies in your toolkit:

**Set the Stage for Success**

* **Optimize the Environment:** Brains struggle to learn when sensory input is overwhelming. Whenever possible, set up your teaching space away from high-traffic areas, loud noises, and visual distractions.  
* **Meet Them Where They Are:** Before you start talking, assess the room. Find out what your group already knows and gauge their current skill level. You do not want to bore the experienced Scouts or completely overwhelm the beginners.  
* **Master the Material (But Be Human):** You need to know how to perform the skill yourself, but you don't have to be flawless. Modeling how to handle a mistake gracefully and try again is one of the most powerful psychological lessons you can teach.

**Brain-Friendly Instruction**

* **Engage Multiple Pathways:** People process information differently. Do not rely entirely on talking. Combine your verbal instructions with visual aids, physical props, and hands-on demonstrations. The more senses you engage, the deeper the memory sticks.  
* **Chunk It Out:** Human working memory has strict limits. Avoid information overload by breaking complex skills into small, digestible "chunks" or steps.  
* **Shrink the Room:** Whenever you can, utilize your junior staff or CITs to break large groups down into smaller clusters. Smaller ratios mean more individualized attention and a lower barrier for Scouts to ask questions.

**Guide the Grapple**

* **Embrace "Productive Struggle":** Set up a learning-by-doing process, step back, and let them try it. Do not immediately rescue a Scout the second they struggle. Grappling with a problem builds resilience and makes the eventual "aha\!" moment much more rewarding.  
* **Foster a Growth Mindset:** When offering encouragement, praise the process, not just the talent. Instead of saying, "You're a natural at this," try, "I love the focus and strategy you used to figure that out." Highlight specific steps they executed well.  
* **Feedback is Fuel, Not Punishment:** When frustration sets in, step in to gently correct obvious mistakes. Keep your tone strictly supportive. Sarcasm, humiliation, or disrespect instantly trigger a stress response in the brain, which literally shuts down a person's ability to learn.

**Empower the Experts**

* **Activate Peer Mentors:** If a Scout catches on exceptionally fast, recruit them on the spot to assist someone who is moving at a slower pace.  
* **The Ultimate Test (Teach It Back):** Once a Scout has mastered a skill, encourage them to teach it to someone else in their troop. Teaching a concept to another person is the absolute best way to solidify that knowledge permanently.

# Darmok and Jalad at Tanagra: A Communication Master Class

Even though we all speak English (presumably), we don't always speak the same “language.” I don't just mean jargon and slang, but we make assumptions about the other person’s intelligence, intentions, or familiarity with a subject. How often has someone told you “I’m fine\!” when you know for a fact that they are not. Human society has taught us to not say what we really mean, even and especially, when it's most important. “Do you need help with that?” “No, I'm fine.” 

How do we overcome this to become the finely tuned staff our scouts deserve? Perhaps a lesson from the 24th century could help. 

Which brings us to something very special (and very nerdy). In the classic Star Trek: The Next Generation episode, “Darmok,” the Enterprise has been assigned to try to establish diplomatic relations with an enigmatic alien race known as Tamarians. The Universal Translator is working, but giving only what seem to be nonsense. Through a series of trials, Picard learns that Dathon, the Tamarian captain, is “speaking in metaphor.” “Darmok and Jalad at Tanagra” refers to a Tamarian legend about two strangers who learned to understand each other, fought a mighty beast, and left together as friends. “Shaka when the walls fell” is a failure, sadness, or simply “no.”

Technically it's actually “memetic abstractions” and “cultural allegories” rather than metaphors, but that's not the point. The lesson is how Picard and Dathon learn to communicate.

**Cutting Through the Alphabet Soup**  
When you rattle off camp lingo, you’re speaking Tamarian. If a senior staffer says, “Go grab the Ranger from the Ogre, stop by STEM in the Commissioner’s Shack and pick up their SOP and bring them to the EMT at the Logs,” a new staffer will stare at you like you’re loony.

* **Be a Cultural Guide:** If you’ve been here before, it’s your responsibility to translate our traditions and look out for the new folks.  
* **Explain the “Why”:** Explain what you’re talking about. I’m not even sure why the Ranger’s tool shed is called The Black Ogre, but that’s what it’s called. The STEM program runs out of what was once the Commissioner’s Shack, and it still clings to that name decades after the last time a Camp Commissioner ever stayed in that cabin. An SOP is a Standard Operating Procedure. EMT is an Emergency Medical Technician, a medical certification our Medic may have. The Logs I explained earlier as the bleachers in front of the Dining Hall.  
* **Reveal the Hidden Map:** Camp has a "hidden curriculum.” Uunspoken rules about where you can sneak some cell signal, what time you can catch the showers when no one is around, what compliments work to tell the Program Director to get out of cleaning the KYBO behind the youth shower house (I’m kidding, that wont work… chocolate though…). Share those secrets freely. Bring the newbies into the cool kid club, don’t gatekeep.

**The El-Adrel Principle (The Power of Shared Toil)**  
In the episode, Picard and Dathon finally make their connection while marooned on El-Adrel Four and facing a camouflaged predator. By facing a physical trial together, they bypass words and build a shared understanding.

Shared labor is a huge equalizer and teams bond best when faced with a common task. Collective physical struggle builds nonverbal coordination and group trust fast. The more you work with different people and the harder the work is, the closer your bond will be, leading to better chemistry through the summer and more productivity all around. 

Perhaps the real El-Adrel is the friends we meet along the way. 

**Assuming Positive Intent (Dealing with Friction)**  
The long days of camp can be hard. You’re going to get frustrated, maybe even get angry and lash out. “Zinda, his face black, his eyes red,” our Tamarian friends would say.

When communication fails here, small gripes can turn into massive headaches. In the episode, Dathon throws a dagger at Picard. A "low-context" response would be to see it as an attack. Picard, however, realized Dathon was offering a tool for their mutual survival.

You have to apply that same grace to your coworkers and campers:

* **Look at the Surroundings:** If someone snaps at you, there’s probably a reason for it. Maybe they’re frustrated about something else, maybe something you did or said hit a nerve you didn’t know, or maybe they just have a headache. .  
* **Truly Listen:** When someone is acting out, look for the root cause rather than getting defensive. Talk to them privately and hear what they’re actually saying.  
* **Speak from the “I”:** Use "I statements" like "When this happens, I feel..." to find solutions together instead of starting a fight. Psychologists recommend this method for a reason. It avoids sounding accusatory and takes responsibility for your side of the situation.

**Reading the Air**  
Memorizing this handbook and all the safety rules won't save you when a camper is in an emotional tailspin. Working with youth requires empathy and the ability to sense what’s unsaid.

* **Identify the Cues:** If a scout is hiding from their troop, they aren’t just being stubborn. They are likely “Darmok on the ocean,” or feeling isolated, homesick, or scared.  
* **Lead with Heart:** Use the U-S-A model: Understand why they’ve pulled away, Speak with them one-on-one, and Act to get them the help they need.

# National Campfire Guidelines

*National Camping School*

Ceremonies and campfires are fun and exciting parts of any camping experience\! They give youth the ability to perform, but run-ons, stories, skits and songs must also fit within the program guidelines of Scouting America. * No bathroom humor or skits/songs where a toilet is the punchline or a part of the skit or song.  
* No water skits – NO ONE gets wet in any way (includes staff, and even if they are “in on it”)

# Campfire Performance Fundamentals

Great campfires come down to great performances with great improv. Embrace the “Yes, and…” philosophy and follow these basic rules:

* **Be Loud & Clear:** If the crowd can't hear you or figure out what you are miming, the joke dies. Enunciate, speak up, and be deliberate with your physical movements.  
* **Keep It Moving:** Don't let a skit overstay its welcome. Build the energy, hit your punchline, and get off the stage before the joke drags.  
* **The Rule of 3:** This is the easiest cheat code for comedy. 1\) Establish a normal scene. 2\) Introduce something weird. 3\) Escalate the weirdness.  
* **Share the Spotlight:** A skit is a team sport. Listen to your scene partners instead of just waiting for your turn to speak, and always pay attention to the crowd's energy.  
* **Embrace Mistakes ("Yes, And"):** If you mess up, pretend you meant to do it. Accept the mistake and weave it straight into the act. The biggest laughs usually come from unscripted chaos.  
* **Commit 100%:** You have to completely buy into your own absurdity. If you break character or look embarrassed, the magic is gone. Go all in.

**The Master of Ceremonies**  
In the case of a Staff Campfire, **don’t MC it**. The staff should know what they’re doing, you don’t need to introduce the next skit. Saturday Night Live doesn’t say “up next, the game show parody and on deck is Weekend Update.” Just keep the surprises coming.

When it comes to the Camper Campfire, we usually have one or two SPL’s do the job, and they’ll need to introduce the next troop and who’s on deck. Don’t say the name of the skit, just in case the name gives away the punchline. Just the troop. **Remember, the Program Director MUST approve all campfire content.**

# How To Lead A Songs

**How to Start a Song**  
Never ask, “Who wants to sing?” or “What do you want to sing?” The crowd will either answer with dead silence or the latest highly inappropriate pop song.

The best way to start a song is to **just start doing it.** Run right up to the crowd and yell as loud as you can: **THIS IS A REPEAT-AFTER-ME SONG\!**

Repeat-After-Me songs are the ultimate cheat code because they practically force participation. Once you have them hooked and warmed up, you can transition into more casual singalongs. Enthusiasm is contagious\! If you look like you are having a blast out there, they will follow your lead.

# Copyright (An Inconvenient Truth)

The reality is, being a summer camp does not protect us from copyright. Legally, printing songs in songbooks is copying and publishing copyright material and singing the songs in camp is  life performance. There’s not many ways around it, and being a summer camp doesn’t legally give us any leeway. We can get permission and/or license the songs, but that’s an awful lot of work you’re probably not going to do. There’s some wiggle room when it comes to fair use, but you’d want to have a chat with a lawyer.

However, the chances of a company like Disney going after Camp Lawton for singing a song in good faith are probably about as high as winning the lottery or getting struck by lightning (while following our hazardous weather guidelines). Realistically, don’t worry about it. BUT… let’s not tempt fate by putting it in writing.

So why do we do it? Because shared absurdity brings people together. It’s deeply psychological. When you are out there sticking your tongue out and buns back during *Tootie-Ta*, you are part of a shared experience. There is a reason churches (and, admittedly, cults) use hymns and rhythmic chants. It opens people up to a communal spirit and makes them more receptive to the program. Scouts who spend a week singing and yelling in unison will leave camp feeling like they had a vastly more meaningful experience than those who just sat on a log waiting for their food.

# The Campfire Arc

Like any great story, a campfire should have an arc. I've heard a number of theories about what it should look like, but I believe the next structure is not entirely unlike the fire itself:

* It should start off with a **bang**, something active, exciting, rousing. If you can do something flashy to light the fire itself, awesome\! Otherwise, just do your liveliest song.   
* Then put some of your best stuff up front. It's your first impression and you gotta give them “permission” to laugh out loud.  
* You can fit the more mundane stuff in the middle. This is your “fun and games” section as Save The Cat (the screenwriting formula by Blake Snyder) would call it. It's not  filler really, but it's where the meat of your campfire lives.   
* Then "throw another log on the fire" with more of your best stuff as the climax. This is the stuff they're going to remember when they go back to their campsite.   
* Then one or two slower but still memorable bits (a meaningful song or story) before your inspirational ending. At Lawton, that's usually Scout Vespers. 

# Campfire Master Class And Songbook

Many of the skits I see today are 50 or more years old. Everyone brings something new to them, which is great, but how many more times do I have to see “Is it time yet?” With Youtube offering endless access to complete libraries of Saturday Night Live, Monty Python, Kids in the Hall, Second City, Groundlings, Upright Citizens Brigade, even BYU’s Studio C, not to mention countless Tiktok/Instagram Reels/YouTube Shorts comedy bits, there’s really no excuse for falling back on the same old jokes.

**But imagine if the next great skit, came from YOU\!** There’s no reason you can’t write a sketch all by yourself that’s funny, appropriate, and topical.

**How To Write Funny**  
*“It’s said that a comic is a person who says funny things but a comedian says things funny. This makes me a juggler.” \-Michael Davis*

***Shamelessly stolen from**: How To Write Funny (series) by Scott Dikkers; Laughing Matters (video) Starring Rowan Atkinson, written by David Hinton, Robin Driscoll, and Rowan Atkinson; The Upright Citizens Brigade Comedy Improvisation Manual by Matt Walsh, Ian Roberts, and Matt Besser*

Phase 1: Finding the Core Concept

* **Brainstorm Relentlessly:** Do not wait for a lightning bolt of inspiration. Make a habit of jotting down funny ideas, random thoughts, or absurd premises every day to build a stockpile of raw material.  
* **The "First Laugh" Test:** A solid skit concept can be pitched in a simple 5-to-10 word sentence. Pitch it to your friends; if the basic premise doesn’t get an immediate "First Laugh," a five-minute skit isn't going to save it.  
* **The "One Weird Thing" Rule:** Your skit should introduce exactly one impossible or absurd thing into a normal world. Throwing in aliens, ghosts, and a talking dog all at once will only confuse the audience. Keep the crazy focused.  
* **The Secret Sauce (Subtext):** The best comedy is grounded in a true observation about human nature or society. Have a point, but whatever you do, do not preach it to the audience.  
* **Keep it Camp-Appropriate:** All concepts must align with the ideals of the Scouting America. Ensure there is no bathroom humor, no sexual overtones, and no portrayals of violent behavior, such as pointing "guns" at each other. The best motto to have is: "If in doubt, take it out\!".

Phase 2: Setting the Stage

* **Build the Base Reality:** Before the comedy starts, establish the "Who, What, and Where." Ground the scene in a totally normal, relatable context so the absurd elements have a solid foundation to bounce off of.  
* **Find "The Game":** Identify the first unusual thing that breaks the normal pattern of your scene. That unusual behavior or situation is the "Game." It is the entire point of the skit.

Phase 3: Mining the Jokes & Physical Comedy

* **The Magic "If/Then":** To generate your jokes, ask yourself: "If this unusual thing is true, then what else is true?"  
* **Riff Your "Joke Beats":** Brainstorm a list of 20 or more ways this absurd situation could play out. These "beats" will form the actual skeleton of your skit, hitting every few lines.  
* **Kill Your Darlings (Joke Discipline):** If a joke is hilarious but veers away from your core premise, cut it. Going off the rails is a rookie mistake that confuses the audience. Stay on your track.  
* **Employ Visual Comedy:** Do not just rely on dialogue; use the three basic principles of visual comedy to elevate the scene: You can create humor by having an object or person behave in an unexpected way, turn up in an unexpected place, or be the completely wrong size.

Phase 4: Building Your Weirdos (Characters)

* **Keep it Two-Dimensional:** You aren't writing Shakespeare. Comedic characters are often just walking human flaws. Rely on classic archetypes (The Know-It-All, The Dummy, The Rule-Follower) and give them one to three extreme traits.  
* **Use Your Body & Wardrobe:** For a physical comedian, the body is the essential means of expression. Use your feet, hands, knees, bottom, and face to portray the whole range of human emotions. Additionally, simply wearing clothes that are slightly too small or slightly too big can psychologically transform a character and generate big laughs.  
* **Avoid Mean-Spiritedness:** Be incredibly careful never to give the appearance that you are making fun of someone. Being mean just isn't funny.  
* **Play it Straight:** Play the absurdity with 100% deadpan sincerity. Do not wink at the audience, do not act "wacky," and do not use stupid cliché names just for a cheap laugh. If you fully believe the reality of the scene, the audience will follow.

Phase 5: Getting It On Paper

* **Turn Up the Dial:** A skit has to escalate. Every new joke beat should raise the stakes, making the scene bigger, crazier, and more absurd than the last one.  
* **Hide the Math (Exposition):** Do not write a boring, three-minute setup just to explain why the scene is happening. Fold the necessary information naturally into the dialogue.  
* **Enter "Clown Mode":** When writing your first draft, turn off your inner critic. String your joke beats together as fast as you can and finish the draft without editing yourself. Let it be messy.

Phase 6: Polishing the Turd 

* **Hit the Button:** End on a high note. Cap the skit with a "button" \- a final, definitive joke that wraps the whole thing up. This is the only time you are allowed to flip the premise upside down for a final surprise.  
* **Enter "Editor Mode":** Walk away for a few days, then come back and aggressively cut the word count. Tighter pacing equals bigger laughs.  
* **Test, Tweak, and Vet:** Show the draft to a test group and rewrite parts that do not land. Finally, all material must be thoroughly vetted by the camp leadership team before performance. Because a skit's title might mean something different to you than it does to someone else, leadership review ensures everything aligns perfectly with the camp's brand.

# Writing Songs

When the Llama Song was written, it began with the 1994 Camp Lawton Cub Scout Camp staff having Na Na Hey Hey (Kiss Him Goodbye) (Steam, 1969\) stuck in their heads. That just naturally evolved into “Llama llama \- I ride a \- ride a llama.” Amidst giggles over the brilliance of that wordplay, staff member Morgan Grantham went away for a bit and wrote the lyrics to the verses as a rap which later became the tune to We Will Rock You (Queen, 1977). That's how great songs are written sometimes. It's just a matter of finding the hook.

When you're leading a troop around the campfire or trying to keep energy up on the logs, you don't have the luxury of a backing track or a synthesizer. The song has to be entirely driven by the group's collective energy.

Here is a cheat sheet for writing a camp song that meets your needs: it demands participation, teaches itself, and sticks in the head long after the fire burns out.   
*(Hat tip to YouTuber NerdSync’s analysis of the DuckTales theme song.)*

**The Melodic Blueprint**

* The Staircase Melody: Keep the core melody restricted to a tight range of notes that move up and down in small, predictable steps. If the notes jump around too frantically, it becomes difficult for an untrained group to sing acapella.  
* The "Mil lennial Whoop" Equivalent: You need a wordless, rhythmic vocalization. The "Woo-oo\!" in DuckTales. Camp songs thrive on these nonsensical, rhythmic syllables (think "Boom Chicka Boom" or "Flea Fly Flow"). They are universally accessible, require zero memorization, and immediately unify the group's timing.

**Structuring for "Zero-Friction" Learning**

* The "One at a Time" Rule: Borrow pop producer Max Martin’s philosophy: never introduce too many elements at once. If the melody is complex, keep the lyrics dead simple. If the lyrics tell a detailed story, keep the melody to a basic chant.  
* Call and Response (The Echo): The absolute easiest way to teach a song without accompaniment is the "Repeat After Me" format. You sing a line, the group echoes it. By the time you reach the chorus, they are already trained on the rhythm and key.  
* The Zipper Structure: Design the verses so that only one word changes each time. For example, "We're going on a bear hunt" simply zippers in a new obstacle (mud, river, grass) while the rest of the framework remains identical. This minimizes the cognitive load on the singers.

**Writing Lyrics that Stick**

* Bookend Rhymes: Rhyming at both the beginning and the end of phrases makes lyrics significantly more memorable. It gives the brain a predictive puzzle to solve, making the next line feel inevitable.  
* Action-Oriented Verbs: Camp songs need kinetic energy. Use words that easily translate to physical movements. If the song mentions rain, there should be a corresponding hand motion. This taps into kinesthetic learning, helping people remember the words through muscle memory.  
* Escalation: A great camp song builds tension or absurdity. It should get faster, louder, or fundamentally weirder with each verse until it culminates in a high-energy, chaotic finale.

**Designing the Acapella "Track"**

* Body Percussion: Since you have no instruments, the arrangement must include built-in percussion. Dictate specific moments for stomps, claps, or lap-slaps. This serves a dual purpose: it keeps the tempo from dragging (a common problem in acapella group singing) and provides an easy participation avenue for those who might be too shy to sing loudly at first.

# Severe Weather Preparedness

**Training Requirements & Compliance**

* Mandatory for Staff: Under National Camp Accreditation Program (NCAP) Standard SQ-402, all camp staff must complete the Hazardous Weather Training online before youth participants arrive. It is also required for all registered Direct Contact Leaders (e.g., Scoutmasters, Cubmasters).  
* Two-Year Renewal: The certification is valid for exactly two years (730 days) and must be kept current.  
* Online Only: The training takes about 30 minutes aynd must be completed individually online via My.Scouting.org. In-person or group-facilitated classes are strictly prohibited and will not be recorded in official training systems.

**Lightning Safety & The 30/30 Rule**

* "When Thunder Roars, Go Indoors": If you can hear thunder, lightning is a danger.  
* The 30-Second Rule: If the time between a lightning flash and the thunder is 30 seconds or less, the storm is within 6 miles. Suspend all outdoor activities immediately and seek shelter.  
* The 30-Minute Rule: Do not leave shelter or resume outdoor activities until at least 30 minutes have passed since the last audible thunder or visible lightning.  
* Safe Shelters: You must use a primary shelter (an enclosed building with a roof, walls, floor, and plumbing/wiring) or a secondary shelter (a fully enclosed hard-topped metal vehicle with rolled-up windows). In our case, the primary lightning and storm shelter is the Dining Hall.  
* Tents, open pavilions, and golf carts offer zero protection from lightning.  
* Caught in the Open: If you cannot reach shelter, avoid tall/isolated trees, water, and metal objects. Disperse the group so individuals are 15 to 100 feet apart, and do NOT lie flat on the ground.

**Heat & Thermal Stress Mitigation**

* Acclimation & Hydration: we are at almost 8,000 ft above sea level and more than 5,000 ft above Tucson. It takes a few days to properly adjust to the decreased air pressure. Be ready for that and prepare scouts for it. The larger issue at Camp Lawton is dehydration. The environment will dehydrate you quickly. You and the scouts should be drinking several liters of water a day. Don't force it, over-hydration is a thing too, but drink when you're thirsty. Alternate it with some electrolyte sports drink if you're having any trouble. If you're feeling a headache, nose bleed, dizzy, and/or fatigued, sit down and relax in the shade for a few minutes sipping your water. If it continues, get to the Health Lodge. Instruct scouts (and leaders) to do the same.  
* Heat Exhaustion vs. Heatstroke:  
  * Heat Exhaustion (pale/clammy skin, heavy sweating, nausea): Move to shade, remove excess clothing, cool with cold water, and hydrate. Do not give salt tablets.  
  * Heatstroke (altered mental state, confusion, extremely hot/flushed skin): This is a life-threatening emergency. Call EMS/911 immediately, initiate rapid cooling (ice packs, cold water immersion), and do not give anything by mouth.  
  * Camp Lawton is in the mountains. It rarely gets much higher than 90°, nothing like what we're used to in the desert, but it's still enough to get sick from the heat. Hypothermia is also not impossible in our environment.

High Winds & Hazard Trees

* Activity Suspension: High winds or microbursts can cause extreme localized damage. Climbing must be suspended immediately if winds exceed predetermined camp limits. We also may shut down Range and Target Activities if the range master determines the wind is unsafe.  
* Hazard Trees ("Widowmakers"): Look out for dead trees, live trees with dead parts, or unstable structures. Never set up tents, hammocks, or program areas within a hazard tree's fall radius. The fall radius on flat ground is 1.5 times the total height of the tree. Management works with the Forest Service each year to bring down hazard trees, but thanks to fires, bark beetles, droughts, and storms, there are bound to still be some trees that may pose a threat. Be aware of these, stay clear, and watch for any leaning trees, signs of the trees swaying more than usual in the wind and listen for cracking sounds. Report these to the Camp Director.

**Camp Emergency Action Plans (EAP) & Alarms**

* Property-Specific EAP: Every camp has a specific, written Emergency Action Plan covering procedures for weather, fires, lost campers, and medical emergencies. All staff must be trained on this and have access to it.  
* Camp Lawton’s EAP: In the event of an emergency, the camp bell will begin ringing continuously. EVERYONE must report directly to the parade grounds (the flags). There we'll do a head count, and proceed with further instructions. We'll give some more detail on this later.

# Safeguarding Youth

Scouting is still recovering from the fallout of some very dark stories. It’s uncomfortable to talk about, but it must be addressed. The important thing to understand is that it’s not about the individual abusers here; it’s about the culture they create that allows the abuse. A scoutmaster doesn’t crawl into the sleeping bag of a youth if there are people around that are unwilling to allow it. Our commitment to Safeguarding Youth is not just about preventing abuse. It’s about making Camp Lawton a place where that could never happen.

The following is a quick reference for the Safeguarding Youth Training content. We will be addressing it during staff training and you will need to complete the online course module annually. These are the most critical rules in the scouting program, and do not allow for any grey area.

It should also be noted that some of the guidelines have changed since the Youth Protection days, so when making decisions, make sure to check that the policy you're remembering is SYT not YPT.

**Requirements**

* Annual Requirement: All staff members and all registered adult scout leaders (18+) must complete the Safeguarding Youth Training annually.  
* Course Structure: The initial course takes 90 minutes. Subsequent annual renewals require a 15–20 minute refresher video and an exam, which must be passed with a score of 80% or higher.  
* Age Transitions: Camp staff members who turn 18 during the season instantly become subject to all adult rules, including separate housing and the prohibition of one-on-one contact with youth.

**Supervision and Contact**

* Two-Deep Leadership: A minimum of two registered adult leaders (21+) is required at all times for all activities. If female youth are present, at least one of the leaders must be a registered female adult.  
* No One-on-One Contact: One-on-one contact between adults (including staff 18+) and youth is strictly prohibited in person, online, via text, or on any digital platform.  
* The Buddy System: Youth must always use the buddy system. Buddy pairs must be single-gender and no more than two years apart in age. Staff members or adults (18+) cannot be buddies with youth members.

**Privacy and Accommodations**

* Separate Accommodations: Male and female youth, as well as youth and adults, must have completely separate tenting and lodging. No adult or staff member over 18 may enter or sleep in youth lodging.  
* Restroom Privacy: Separate shower and latrine facilities must be provided for adults and youth, and for males and females. Camp Lawton has designated adult and youth shower houses, and each has individual private facilities. Staff Hill also has individual private facilities with locks for showers and toilets.  
* Respect of Privacy: Adults may only intrude on youth privacy for health and safety emergencies. The use of cameras, smartphones, mirrors, or drones in areas where privacy is expected (like showers or changing areas) is strictly prohibited.

**Behavior and Discipline**

* Constructive Discipline: Discipline must always be constructive, positive, and reflect Scouting's values. Corporal punishment, isolation, humiliation, and ridicule are strictly prohibited.  
* Zero Tolerance: There is a strict zero-tolerance policy for hazing, initiations, secret organizations, bullying, cyberbullying, or physical violence.  
* No Sexual Activity: All sexual activity and inappropriate public displays of affection are strictly prohibited at camp.  
* Appropriate Attire: Modest and proper clothing is required at all times. For example, swimwear must be modest (e.g., no tight-fitting swim briefs or bikinis).

**Incident Response and Mandatory Reporting**

* Intervene Immediately: If you witness abuse, policy violations, or unsafe behavior, you must step in immediately to stop it. Do not dismiss inappropriate actions as "just kids being kids".  
* Confidentiality: Any information concerning campers is strictly confidential. This is of the utmost importance when dealing with suspected cases of child abuse. The camper must never be informed of this notification process. His/her knowledge of this report could endanger him/her when he/she returns home.  
* The Perpetrator: If an alleged perpetrator is on camp grounds, they will be immediately separated from the campers until the incident is resolved, until the threat is removed, or as long as necessary to protect the safety and welfare of the campers.  
* Mandatory Reporters: All camp staff and volunteers are mandated reporters. This duty cannot be delegated to anyone else.  
* Reporting Chain: You must immediately report any good-faith suspicion of abuse or policy violations to the Camp Director. At that point, both of you are responsible for contacting the necessary authorities. You will need to speak to DCS as described below while the Camp Director contacts the Scout Executive. The reason for this painful process is to prevent any chance or accusations of institutional concealment by the organization. Don't be afraid of this, you're doing the right thing.  
* Scouts First Helpline: Scouting America offers the 24-hour Scouts First Helpline at 1-844-SCOUTS1 (1-844-726-8871) in the event you need immediate assistance regarding a youth safety concern and the Camp Director is unavailable.  
* Immediate Risk: Call 911 immediately if someone is at immediate risk of harm.

**The Law**  
Mandatory Reporting (ARS 13-3620) As a camp staff member, YOU are a mandated reporter. This isn't just a Scouting policy; it is Arizona State Law (ARS 13-3620). YOU (not the Camp Director or Council Executive) are legally required to report all concerns of child abuse or neglect to The Arizona Department of Child Safety (DCS). Reach out to the 24/7 state hotline by calling 1-888-SOS-CHILD (888-767-2445). In a life-threatening crisis, skip the hotline and dial 911 immediately. While mandated reporters can submit non-emergency details via the Guardian online portal, the simple reality is that every citizen is legally required to report suspected abuse without delay to the Department of Child Safety or the local authorities.

**Legal Definitions you need to know**

1. Child Abuse: Harm or threatened harm to a child's health or welfare, which occurs through non-accidental physical or mental injury, sexual abuse, or maltreatment.  
2. Child Neglect: Harm to a child's health or welfare by a person responsible for them, which occurs through negligent treatment (failure to provide adequate food, clothing, shelter, emotional, or medical care).

# Missing Person (Code Blue)

When a missing person is reported, all normal camp operations pause. Locating the individual immediately becomes the single highest priority for the entire staff until the "all clear" is given.

If a camper or staff member is reported missing to you, follow these steps in order:

1. **Gather Critical Details**: The first staff member made aware of the situation must quickly obtain an accurate profile of the missing individual. You need:  
   1. First and last name (and age, if known)  
   2. Troop/Unit number  
   3. Physical description (specifically height, hair color, and what they are currently wearing)   
   4. Last known location and the time they were last seen  
   5. Any known medical needs or behavioral tendencies (e.g., if they were upset before wandering off)  
2. **Initiate "Code Blue":** Strict radio discipline is vital during this phase. Immediately contact the Camp Director over the radio, declare a "Code Blue," and clearly relay the gathered details. Keep the channel clear of all non-emergency traffic.  
3. **Search Coordination**: While all staff must immediately stand by and prepare to respond, the actual physical search will be organized and directed \*exclusively\* by the Camp Director or their designated search leader. Centralizing command ensures the search is systematic, prevents duplicated efforts, and keeps well-meaning staff from accidentally trampling over tracks or clues.   
4. **Camp-Wide Headcount**: At the Camp Director’s discretion, the Emergency Bell may be rung to initiate a complete camp headcount, lock down unit movements, and account for all remaining individuals on the property.

# Bear & Wildlife Safety

Camp Lawton is located within active black bear and mountain lion habitat. While sightings are rare, it takes very little for a wildlife encounter to escalate. Strict, uncompromising adherence to our camp policies is the single most effective method for preventing these incidents.

#### 

**Prevention: The "Smellables" Policy**  
Bears have an extraordinary sense of smell and are driven by food conditioning.

* **The Rule:** Absolutely no food, drink, or "smellables" (deodorant, toothpaste, lotions, ChapStick, and other scented products) are allowed anywhere in camp except in the immediate vicinity of the Dining Hall. The *only* exception is plain water.  
* **Overnight Storage:** All smellables must be secured in the designated "Bear Box" and transported to the Smellables Shed (located next to the youth shower house) every single night.  
* **Enforcement:** If you see anyone—staff or Scout—with food or scented items outside of allowed areas, politely but firmly instruct them to relocate the items to the proper storage area immediately. Report persistent issues to senior staff.

#### 

**Protocol for Bear Sightings (Code Brown)**  
If you spot a bear in camp, remain calm. Black bears will typically ignore you or retreat if you make your presence known and establish yourself as a human.

* **Report:** Immediately report the sighting to the Camp Director over the radio as a "Code Brown," providing your exact location.  
* **Observe:** If it is entirely safe to do so, adult staff should maintain a distant visual on the animal so camp administration can track its movements away from high-traffic areas.  
* **Official Response:** Do not attempt to manage the wildlife yourself. The Camp Director will handle all required communication with the Arizona Game and Fish Department.

#### 

**In the Event of an Attack**  
In the highly unlikely event of a black bear attack, the immediate priority is to aggressively scare the animal away from the victim. (Note: You *fight back* against black bears; never play dead).

* **Scare the Bear:** Yell loudly, wave your arms to appear large, and throw rocks or heavy objects. Do not approach the bear directly.  
* **Call for Help:** Immediately dispatch a runner to locate the Camp Director and the Ranger.  
* **First Aid & Assembly:** As soon as the animal retreats, begin treating the victim. The Camp Director will sound the emergency alarm (continuous bell). All Scouts and staff who are not actively treating the victim must report immediately to the Parade Grounds for a lockdown and headcount.

#### 

**Other Potentially Dangerous Wildlife**  
Camp Lawton shares its environment with other native species, including turkeys, rattlesnakes, black widow spiders, and occasionally mountain lions (primarily in the winter, though summer sightings have occurred).

* **General Rule:** Never approach, corner, or attempt to handle wildlife. Give them a wide berth and observe from a safe distance.  
* **Mountain Lions:** If you see a mountain lion, do not run. Stand your ground, make yourself look as large as possible, and back away slowly while keeping your eyes on the animal. Talk to it, let it know you’re human and not suitable food, but **do not be threatening.** They generally do not have much interest in humans (this is one reason why pets are prohibited).  
* **Venomous Critters:** If a rattlesnake or black widow is discovered in a high-traffic area, campsite, or building, clear the immediate area and notify the Camp Director or Ranger for safe removal. Do not attempt to kill or relocate it yourself.  
* **Turkeys:** Turkeys are relative newcomers to camp during the camper season, but are welcome. Enjoy the opportunity to observe such impressive animals, but they *can* still cause harm if you do something dumb like attempt to approach and touch it. They are still wild animals and will defend themselves if provoked.

# Wildfires

Wildfires are very much a reality in this environment, making strict adherence to fire safety and emergency communication protocols **essential**.

* **Campfire Restrictions** \- All campfires are subject to current Forest Service regulations, which change without notice. Administration will monitor the status each day, and this app has the latest alerts at the top if you have an active internet connection. These restrictions also apply to fireplaces in camp, but generally not to gas fires (although those need to be closely monitored as well).    
* **Fire in Camp (Immediate Threat) \-** If you observe a wildfire or an out-of-control campfire within the camp, **immediately** contact a member of the camp administration or senior staff. The **camp alarm** will sound so the threat can be evaluated and addressed in a safe and orderly manner.  
* **Distant Fires (Monitoring Phase) \-** If there is a wildfire **on the mountain** that does not pose an **immediate threat** to the camp, your job is to **remain calm** and keep the Scouts relaxed. # Lightning & Severe Thunderstorms

Mountain weather can turn quickly. When severe storms approach, strict adherence to these protocols ensures everyone's safety.

* **The 30-Second Rule (Danger Threshold):** If the time between seeing a lightning flash and hearing the thunder is 30 seconds or less, lightning is striking within a dangerous proximity (roughly 6 miles). **All outdoor activities must cease immediately.**

**Shelter Protocols**

* **Primary Action:** If it is safe to travel, all staff and campers should relocate directly to the Dining Hall and remain inside until the official "all clear" is given.  
* **Shelter in Place:** If you are already in a secure, enclosed structure and it is unsafe to travel across camp, stay exactly where you are. Immediately radio the Camp Director to report your location and headcount.

**Staff Assignments & Movement**

* **Wait for Orders:** Camp administration may need to dispatch staff to check on specific troop campsites. **Do not self-assign.** Never take on this responsibility without a direct order from a supervisor. Ensure leadership knows your exact whereabouts at all times.  
* **Movement Hazards:** If you are officially dispatched to move through camp during a storm, you must strictly avoid tall poles, large isolated trees, and open high ground.

**Escalation & Evacuation**

* **Mandatory Evacuation:** If lightning strikes dangerously close and poses an immediate threat to the campsites, the camp alarm (continuous bell) will sound. This triggers a mandatory, immediate evacuation of all campsites to the Dining Hall.  
* **Unit Prerogative:** Prior to the alarm sounding, individual troops always maintain the right to seek heavier shelter at the Dining Hall if they feel their canvas tents are inadequate for the current weather conditions.

# Armed Intruder / Active Shooter

While an active shooter event is highly unlikely, it requires a completely different response than weather or wildlife emergencies. Because this is a dynamic, human-driven threat, strict step-by-step procedures cannot cover every variable.

If you hear gunshots or receive a radio warning of an armed intruder, assume an immediate threat to life. Your only goal is the survival of yourself and the Scouts in your care. Assess your immediate surroundings and execute the best option using the **Run, Hide, Fight** protocol:

* **Run (Evacuate):** If there is an accessible escape path, move yourself and the Scouts away from the threat immediately. In a camp setting, this often means abandoning the trails and fleeing deep into the woods, away from main roads and core buildings. Leave all personal belongings behind and keep moving until you are absolutely certain you are out of the danger zone.  
* **Hide (Conceal & Secure):** If evacuation is impossible or you are unsure where the threat is, find a place to hide. If you are in a building, lock and barricade the doors, close the blinds, turn off the lights, and stay away from windows. If you are outdoors, use the natural terrain for cover (large boulders, thick brush, or deep ravines). Turn down your radio volume, silence all cell phones, and require absolute, unbroken quiet from the Scouts.  
* **Fight (Defend):** This is the absolute last resort. If your life or the lives of the Scouts are in imminent, unavoidable danger, you must disrupt or incapacitate the shooter. Act with extreme physical aggression, use improvised weapons (camp tools, heavy rocks, fire extinguishers), and commit entirely to your actions to survive.

**Critical Notes During an Event:**

* **Do not sound the camp bell.** A general assembly alarm will draw people into the open and create a concentrated target.  
* **Do not hunt the threat.** Do not attempt to be a hero or track down the shooter. Your sole responsibility is to protect the youth and get to safety.  
* **Communicate only if safe:** If you are safely hidden and can do so without giving away your position, use your radio or cell phone to quietly relay the shooter's location, direction of movement, and description to the Camp Director or 911\.

# Full Evacuation

In the event a fire or other emergency situation forces an evacuation of camp, the alarm will sound and instructions will be given. Specifics will depend on the situation, but here are the key things to know:

No one else should give instructions that could conflict with the instructions of the Camp Director. It is critical everyone be on the same page.

* All leaders and staff with vehicles should carry their keys with them at all times.   
* The Camp Director, Program Director, and the Ranger will be the last out of camp ensuring everyone in camp has been accounted for and out of camp safely.

# Fatality Protocol

In the tragic event of a fatality at camp, the absolute priorities are immediate scene preservation, respect for the deceased, and strictly limiting camper exposure to minimize trauma.  
If you are the first to respond to or discover a fatality, follow these steps immediately:

* **1\. Immediate Reporting:** Notify the first available Area Director or senior staff member immediately. They will take charge of the area and instantly radio the Camp Director and the Medical Officer.  
* **2\. Secure & Shield the Area:**  
  * **Clear Onlookers:** Immediately remove all campers, adult leaders, and non-essential staff from the visual vicinity. Direct them to a designated, supervised area away from the scene.  
  * **Cover the Individual:** Staff should respectfully cover the deceased using a blanket, tarp, or whatever is immediately available to ensure privacy and prevent bystanders from viewing the scene.  
* **3\. Preserve the Scene:** Treat the area with the strictest preservation standards. **Touch nothing and move nothing.** Until law enforcement and the coroner arrive, the area must not be disturbed. Leave all equipment, personal items, and the surrounding environment exactly as you found them.  
* **4\. Leadership Handover & Communications:** Once senior staff and the Camp Director arrive, they will take exclusive control of the physical scene. The Camp Director will handle all official communications with authorities and families. Under no circumstances should staff communicate about the event over radios (beyond the initial call) or personal cell phones.  
* **5\. Trauma Support:** An incident of this nature is highly traumatic for the entire camp community. Camp administration will arrange for professional counseling and mental health support services for both campers and staff as soon as possible.

# Unexpected Incidents

If you encounter a strange, disruptive, or dangerous situation that is not covered by standard emergency protocols, follow these fundamental steps:

3. **Assess & Adapt:** Wait for leadership to arrive. The situation will be assessed on the ground and managed according to the best judgment of camp administration.  
4. **Stand By for Instructions:** Keep the area secure, project a calm demeanor for the Scouts, and follow whatever ad-hoc instructions are issued by senior management.

* **Avoid "No Comment":** Do not use the phrase "no comment," as it often sounds defensive or implies you are hiding something. Instead, politely say something like, *"I'm not authorized to speak on behalf of the camp, but let me help you find the Camp Director who can answer your questions."*  
* **Official Channels Only:** All interviews and official statements will be handled exclusively through camp administration. This ensures facts are accurate and the situation is not misrepresented in a story. The Camp Director will be present in any situation that you are authorized to speak to the press.  
* **Model the Oath and Law:** Always be respectful, friendly, and courteous. Even if you cannot answer their questions, you are still actively representing the camp and the wider Scouting movement to the public. Make it a positive interaction.

# Incident Response Protocols

Whether dealing with broken equipment or a medical emergency, rapid assessment and clear communication are essential.  
**Property Damage**

* **Minor Damage:** As soon as damage is discovered or reported, inspect it to determine if it poses an immediate safety hazard. If it is dangerous, post someone to guard the area and notify the Camp Director immediately. If it is safe, report it to the Camp Director and Camp Ranger at your earliest convenience.  
  * *Note on Accidents:* If you accidentally caused the damage, do not let fear prevent you from reporting it. Accidents happen; as long as there was no malicious intent, you will not be punished. Unreported damage, however, can escalate into a safety hazard.  
* **Major Damage:** Do not attempt to repair severe damage yourself. Immediately secure the area to ensure no one is in danger, document the details of the damage, and report it directly to the Camp Director and Camp Ranger. Be prepared to complete an official written incident report.

**Medical Incidents & Injuries**

* **Minor Injuries:** Campers should report minor injuries to their adult unit leader, who can escort them to the Health Lodge for treatment. Staff should help evaluate the injured person's mobility. If there is *any* doubt about the extent of the injury or their ability to walk safely, **do not move them**. Send a runner for first aid instead.  
* **Major Injuries:**  
  * **Do Not Move the Victim:** Under no circumstances should staff or participants attempt to move a severely injured person unless they are in immediate, life-threatening danger (e.g., a spreading fire).  
  * **Secure & Comfort:** Ensure the immediate area is safe from further hazards. Keep the victim as comfortable and calm as possible. **Never leave an injured party alone at any time.**  
  * **Call for Help:** Dispatch a runner immediately to locate the on-duty Medical Officer and notify the Camp Director.  
  * **Scene Management:** Once medical personnel arrive, stay out of their way but remain on the scene. Make yourself available to act as a runner or to manage crowd control, keeping onlookers and Scouts away until the emergency is fully resolved and you are officially relieved by senior staff.

# Camp Opening Procedures

**Pre-Camp Training**  
As of 2026, the National Camp Accreditation Program (NCAP) standards outline specific pre-camp training requirements based on the type of camp, alongside mandatory certifications for all camp staff.

**Training Hour Requirements**

**Long-Term Camps:** Staff must receive a **minimum of 28 hours of training**. This training must focus on the development of outdoor skills, leadership, and teaching, and it must be strictly instructional—time spent on the physical setup of the camp (such as pitching tents or cleaning buildings) does not count toward these 28 hours. The training must be supervised by personnel trained at a National Camping School (NCS). If the camp offers multiple types of long-term programs, an extra 10 hours of training is required for each additional program type. It is highly recommended that a full day of this training takes place 30 to 60 days before the camp opens to begin preparing staff for their responsibilities.

**Mandatory Pre-Camp Courses and Certifications** All camp staff and personnel must complete the following specific training modules as part of their preparation:

* **Youth Protection Training:** Must be completed before or at camp and renewed every two years.  
* **Understanding and Preventing Peer on Peer Abuse:** Online training must be completed annually, and certificates must be retained by the camp.  
* **Hazardous Weather Training:** Required for all staff and must be renewed every two years.  
* **Workplace Harassment Prevention for Employees:** This annual online training includes reading and agreeing to the Scouter Code of Conduct. At long-term camps, this applies to all staff and personnel; at day camps, it is required specifically for all paid staff and personnel.  
* **First Aid and CPR/AED:** At least 50% of the staff must be currently certified in American Red Cross Adult CPR/AED and First Aid (or an approved equivalent) to ensure a minimum ratio of one trained staff member for every 25 campers.

**Staff Week**  
Preceding the first week of campers, there will be a mandatory training and preparation period (lasting 2 to 6 days, depending on camp needs and management discretion). You must attend this week in its entirety unless you have made prior arrangements with the Camp Director.

**Area Safety & Risk Identification**  
While the Maintenance Committee spends many hours preparing the facilities and anticipating hazards before the season begins, **Area Directors are ultimately responsible for carefully examining their own program areas for potential risks.**

* **Identify & Mark Hazards:** Directors must look for, record, and mark any hazards. These can include unsafe trails, dangerous trees, bad fencing, broken floors, or other nuisances.  
* **Aesthetic Issues:** Directors should also look for and correct aesthetic problems in their area, such as trash piles or ugly/broken tables.  
* **Correction Plan:** Once a hazard is identified, a plan to correct or manage it will be established based on the combined recommendations of the Area Director, Camp Director, and Camp Ranger.

**Equipment & Inventory Management**  
Accurate inventory tracking is crucial. Throughout the summer, Area Directors are responsible for the safe use, care, and tracking of all equipment in their area. All camp equipment must be accounted for.

* **Early Preparation:** It is highly recommended to arrange to come up to camp early to identify your supply needs before the rush begins.  
* **Opening Inventory:** Conducting a complete inventory of your area's current supplies is one of the first mandatory projects during Staff Week. Compare your count to last year's inventory (if available), and quickly identify any missing supplies so they can be ordered immediately.  
* **Ongoing Tracking:** Keep your inventory updated continuously throughout the summer. This ensures supplies are replenished efficiently as needed and makes the end-of-season process much easier.  
* **Closing Inventory:** At the end of the summer, the Area Director must complete a final closing inventory. This must include a record of all supplies being left behind, exact storage locations for any equipment stored elsewhere, and recommendations for what needs to be ordered for the following year. Good documentation ensures next year's staff is ready to go early.

**Program Compliance & Reviews**

* **3-Year Activity Review Cycle:** Per national policy, camp administration is required to review all camp activities and programs at least every three years.  
* **Program Hazard Analysis (PHA):** A written analysis must be updated every 5 years for any camp activity that is not covered by a standard merit badge pamphlet.  
* **Call and Response:** For any safety-critical tasks (such as rock climbing activities), Scouting America requires a strict verbal “Call and Response” check (e.g., “on belay” / “belay on”).

**Forest Service Permitting**  
Catalina Council and the Camp Director are responsible for ensuring we meet all obligations of our Forest Service lease. This includes:

* Coordinating all major facility work through the Forest Service.  
* Securing all proper permitting.  
* Strictly banning all paramilitary maneuvers, gambling, and the storage of hazardous waste anywhere on the property.

# Camp Lawton Staff Code of Conduct

The Scout Oath and Law are the foundation of our camp culture. As a Camp Lawton staff member, your personal habits and actions are the living embodiment of Scouting. You agree to align your conduct, both on and off the clock, with the ideals of Scouting America, ensuring you remain a positive role model and maintain the physical and mental readiness required to serve our campers.

To maintain a safe, highly functional, and supportive camp environment, we rely on a shared standard of behavior.

**Our Support & Accountability Framework**  
We recognize that working at camp is a high-pressure environment. Mistakes happen, and our primary goal is to help you grow and succeed. However, consistent failure to meet standards negatively impacts the campers and your fellow staff. We address behavioral and performance issues through a progressive support model:

* **Phase 1:** Coaching & Realignment (The Check-In): For initial or minor lapses in judgment, appearance, or performance. Your Area Director will discuss the issue with you, identify any underlying causes (e.g., fatigue, lack of training), and clearly outline the expected change.  
* **Phase 2:** Formal Intervention (The Action Plan): For repeated issues or more significant breaches of conduct. You will meet with camp administration to create a documented plan for improvement. At this stage, youth staff will have their parents or guardians notified.  
* **Phase 3:** Separation (Dismissal): If the intervention plan is not followed, or if a staff member proves fundamentally incompatible with the camp's culture and expectations, their employment will be terminated.

**Daily Behavioral Expectations**  
Rather than listing every possible mistake, staff are expected to proactively uphold the following standards:

1. **Professionalism & Engagement**  
   1. **Be Present:** Arrive on time to all meals, assemblies, and program areas. Sleeping on the job, leaving your assigned area without permission, or abandoning your duties creates a safety hazard and places an unfair burden on your team.  
   2. **Stay Sharp:** Maintain excellent personal hygiene, adhere to the uniform guidelines at all times, and keep your living quarters and assigned facilities clean and organized.  
   3. **Respect the Hierarchy:** Follow the instructions of your supervisors and the camp administration. If you disagree with a directive, comply first (unless it is unsafe) and use the open-door grievance policy to discuss it afterward.  
2. Respect for the Community  
   1. **Assume Positive Intent:** Treat everyone with the courtesy and kindness outlined in the Scout Law. Abusive language, obscene gestures, bullying, hazing, and malicious pranks have no place at Camp Lawton.  
   2. **Maintain Boundaries:** Respect the privacy of others. Never enter another cabin without permission, and strictly adhere to all Safeguarding Youth guidelines regarding one-on-one contact and appropriate supervision.  
   3. **Honor the Environment:** Care for camp equipment and property as if it were your own. Unauthorized use of camp materials or driving vehicles in camp without express permission from the Ranger and Camp Director is prohibited.  
3. **Integrity & Focus**  
   1. **Media Standards:** Any media consumed in camp must align with Scouting values. Staff may not view R-rated movies or play M-rated video games on camp property.  
   2. **Financial Integrity:** Staff may not accept gratuities, bribes, or tips from campers or visitors, nor may they sell personal items or services on camp property.  
   3. **Authorized Visitors:** All visitors must be approved in advance by the Camp Director and follow all sign-in procedures to ensure camp security.

**Zero-Tolerance: Immediate Discharge**  
While we prioritize coaching, certain actions pose an immediate threat to the physical, emotional, or legal safety of our campers, staff, and organization. Involvement in any of the following will result in immediate dismissal, removal from camp property, and, when applicable, reporting to law enforcement and the Department of Child Safety:

1. **Safety & Abuse Violations**: Any violation of Safeguarding Youth policies, including sexual or physical harassment, abuse, hazing, or any sexual activities on camp property.  
2. **Substance Violations**: The possession, use, or being under the influence of alcohol, illicit drugs, or unprescribed medications. (Smoking or vaping by anyone under 21 is strictly prohibited; authorized use by those 21+ is heavily restricted).  
3. **Theft & Destruction**: Stealing personal or camp property, gambling, or willful destruction/vandalism of camp facilities.  
4. **Contraband**: Possession or distribution of pornography, firearms, or any items listed on the "Strictly Prohibited" list.  
5. **Falsification**: Misrepresenting information on your application, medical forms, or official incident reports.  
6. **Endangerment**: Transporting a minor in a vehicle without written parental permission filed directly with the Camp Director.

I have read and agreed to live by this Code of Conduct, the Camp Staff Guide, my Letter of Agreement, the Scout Oath, and the Scout Law. I understand the camp administration reserves the right to update this document, and I agree to abide by any future changes provided to me.
